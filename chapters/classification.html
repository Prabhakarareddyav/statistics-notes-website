3.1 Introduction

Raw data or ungrouped data are usually unorganized and difficult to interpret. Therefore, it is necessary to arrange the data into a systematic and meaningful form so that statistical analysis becomes easier.

Classification helps in condensing large masses of data into a clear and understandable structure.

3.2 Definition

Classification is the process of arranging individual data items into different groups or classes according to their common characteristics or similarities.

Examples:
  • Letters in a post office classified according to destination cities.
  • Population classified as male and female.
  • Plants classified according to height.

Classification based on:

  • One characteristic → One-way classification
  • Two characteristics → Two-way classification
  • More than two characteristics → Multi-way classification

3.3 Objectives / Advantages of Classification

The major objectives are:

  • Condenses large amounts of data.
  • Removes unnecessary details.
  • Provides orderly arrangement of data.
  • Facilitates comparison between groups.
  • Helps in drawing conclusions.
  • Helps in tabulation and further statistical analysis.

3.4 Types of Classification

Statistical data can be classified into four main types:

  • Chronological Classification
  • Geographical Classification
  • Qualitative Classification
  • Quantitative Classification

1. Chronological Classification

In this classification, data are arranged according to time such as days, months, or years.

Example: Food grain production in India.

Year Production (million tonnes)
2005–06 208.60
2006–07 217.28
2007–08 230.78
2008–09 234.47

2. Geographical Classification

In this classification, data are arranged according to geographical locations such as states, districts, countries, or rural and urban areas.

Example: Food grain production by state.

State Production (tonnes)
Orissa 300000
A.P 250000
U.P 2200000
Assam 10000

3. Qualitative Classification

In qualitative classification, data are grouped according to attributes or qualities that cannot be measured numerically.

Examples:

  • Sex (Male / Female)
  • Religion
  • Employment status
  • Education

Simple (Dichotomous) Classification

Classification based on only one attribute. Example: Population → Male / Female

Manifold Classification

When classification is based on two or more attributes. Example: Population → Sex → Education → Income

4. Quantitative Classification

Quantitative classification is based on measurable characteristics.

Examples include:
  • Height
  • Weight
  • Income
  • Marks
  • Age

Example: Classification of students by height.

Height (cm) No. of Students
100–125 20
125–150 25
150–175 40
175–200 15
