6.1 Introduction

Statistical results can be presented effectively using diagrams and graphs. A single diagram can represent data more clearly than many words. Even people who are not familiar with numerical data can easily understand diagrams.

Diagrams are geometrical figures such as lines, bars, rectangles, circles, pictures and maps used to visually represent statistical data.

Diagrammatic representation highlights relationships and important facts in the data and helps save time and effort in understanding large datasets.

---

6.2 Advantages of Diagrams

  • They are attractive and impressive.
  • They simplify complex data.
  • They help in easy comparison.
  • They save time and labour.
  • They have universal utility.
  • They provide more information visually.
  • They have strong memorizing effect.
---

6.3 Demerits / Limitations

  • They provide only approximate values.
  • Small differences may not be visible clearly.
  • Large differences may distort the appearance.
  • Some diagrams require expert skills.
  • Different scales may mislead viewers.
  • Similar data characteristics are needed for comparison.
  • Limited use for advanced statistical analysis.
---

6.4 Types of Diagrams

Diagrams can be classified into the following categories:

  • One-Dimensional Diagrams
  • Two-Dimensional Diagrams
  • Three-Dimensional Diagrams
  • Pictograms and Cartograms
---

6.5 One-Dimensional Diagrams

In these diagrams only length or height is considered while width is ignored. They are commonly represented using bars or lines.

Types

  • Line Diagram
  • Simple Bar Diagram
  • Sub-divided Bar Diagram
  • Percentage Bar Diagram
  • Multiple Bar Diagram
---

Line Diagram

A line diagram is used when many items need to be shown with small differences. Vertical lines are drawn based on scale.

Example: Number of Children
No. of Children 0 1 2 3 4 5
Frequency 10 14 9 6 4 2
---

Simple Bar Diagram

Simple bar diagrams compare values of a single variable for different categories. Bars may be vertical or horizontal and their heights represent magnitude.

Example: Population in States (million)
Year UP AP MH
1951 63.22 31.25 29.98
---

Sub-divided Bar Diagram

In a sub-divided bar diagram the total bar is divided into parts representing different components of the total.

Example: Family Expenditure
Item Family A (Rs) Family B (Rs)
Food 1400 2400
House Rent 1600 2600
Education 1200 1600
Savings 800 1400
Total 5000 8000
---

Percentage Bar Diagram

Each bar represents 100% and components are shown as percentages of the total.

Formula:

Percentage = (Actual Value / Total Value) × 100

---

Multiple Bar Diagram

Multiple bar diagrams compare two or more related sets of data. Bars are placed side by side for easy comparison.

---

6.6 Two-Dimensional Diagrams

These diagrams represent data using area. Both length and width are used.

Types

  • Rectangle Diagram
  • Square Diagram
  • Circle Diagram
  • Pie Diagram
---

Pie Diagram

Pie diagrams represent total values as a circle divided into sectors representing components.

Formula for Angle:

Angle = (Component Value / Total Value) × 360°

Example: Cropping Pattern in Karnataka
Crop Area (ha) Angle
Cereals 3940 214°
Oil Seeds 1165 63°
Pulses 464 25°
Cotton 249 13°
Others 822 45°
Total 6640 360°
---

6.7 Three-Dimensional Diagrams

These diagrams represent data using volume and include length, width and height. Examples include:

  • Cubes
  • Cylinders
  • Spheres
---

6.8 Pictogram and Cartogram

Pictogram

A pictogram represents data using pictures or symbols proportional to the data values.

Cartogram

Cartograms present statistical data on maps using colors, symbols or patterns. They are commonly used to represent geographical data such as population, production, birth rates and trade statistics.
