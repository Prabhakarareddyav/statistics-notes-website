7.1 Introduction

Graphical presentation of data is an effective way of representing statistical information. Graphs help to understand relationships between variables easily and clearly. Compared with tables, graphs provide a more attractive and visual representation of data.

A graph shows the relationship between two or more sets of data using points and lines. It allows comparisons and helps even non-experts understand statistical information.

---

7.2 Difference Between Graph and Diagram

Sl No Diagram Graph
1 Represented using pictures such as bars, squares, circles etc. Represented using points and lines.
2 Can be drawn on any paper. Requires graph paper.
3 Cannot locate measures like median or mode. Can help locate median and mode.
4 Used for categorical or geographical data. Used for time series and frequency distribution.
5 Shows approximate idea. Shows exact information.
6 More impressive visually. Less impressive visually.
7 Creates lasting impression. Less lasting effect.
---

7.3 Advantages of Graphical Representation

  • Facilitates comparison between variables.
  • Shows relationship or correlation between variables.
  • Helps analyze effects of various factors.
  • Useful for forecasting future trends.
  • Assists in statistical analysis and research planning.
  • More useful than diagrams for time-series and frequency data.
---

7.4 Limitations

  • Graphs cannot show all details available in tables.
  • Graphs give approximate values only.
  • Drawing graphs requires more time.
  • Graphs may not show accuracy of data.
---

Types of Graphical Representation

Graphs are mainly used for two statistical series:

  • Time Series Data
  • Frequency Distribution
---

7.5 Time Series Graph (Historigram)

Time-series graphs show data over time. Time is plotted on the X-axis and the variable value on the Y-axis.

1. One Variable Historigram

Only one variable is plotted against time. Points are joined by straight lines to show trends.

2. Historigram for Two or More Variables

Two or more variables measured in the same units can be plotted on the same graph. Different line styles are used for each variable.

3. Historigram with Two Scales

When variables have different units, two vertical scales are used (left Y-axis and right Y-axis).

4. Belt Graph (Band Curve)

A belt graph shows total values divided into components for each time period. The regions between lines are shaded with colors.

5. Range Graph

A range graph shows maximum and minimum values for each period. Example: daily maximum and minimum temperature.

---

7.6 Frequency Distribution Graphs

Frequency distributions can be represented using several types of graphs:

  • Histogram
  • Frequency Polygon
  • Frequency Curve
  • Ogives (Cumulative Frequency Curve)
---

Histogram

Histogram is the most widely used graph for frequency distributions. It consists of adjacent rectangles where:

  • Width = Class Interval
  • Height = Frequency

Class intervals are shown on the X-axis and frequencies on the Y-axis.

Construction Steps

  1. Convert inclusive classes to exclusive if necessary.
  2. Plot class intervals on X-axis.
  3. Plot frequencies on Y-axis.
  4. Draw rectangles with heights equal to frequencies.
  5. Rectangles must touch each other.
---

Histogram for Unequal Class Intervals

When class intervals are unequal, frequency density is used instead of frequency.

Frequency Density = Frequency / Class Width

---

Difference Between Histogram and Bar Diagram

Histogram Bar Diagram
Two-dimensional diagram One-dimensional diagram
Bars touch each other Bars separated by gaps
Used for frequency distributions Used for categorical data
---

Frequency Polygon

A frequency polygon is obtained by joining the midpoints of the tops of histogram rectangles.

Alternatively, points representing class midpoints and frequencies are joined by straight lines.

---

Frequency Curve

A frequency curve is similar to a frequency polygon but uses a smooth curve instead of straight lines.

---

Ogives (Cumulative Frequency Curve)

An ogive is a graph of cumulative frequencies. It is used to determine values like median, quartiles, deciles, and percentiles.

Types of Ogives

  • Less Than Ogive – cumulative frequencies plotted using upper class limits.
  • More Than Ogive – cumulative frequencies plotted using lower class limits.

The intersection of these two curves gives the median graphically.

---

Advantages of Ogive Curve

  • Helps compute median, quartiles, and percentiles graphically.
  • Shows number of observations below or above certain values.
  • Useful for comparing distributions.
