2.1 Data

Data are numerical observations collected in a systematic manner by assigning numbers or scores to the outcomes of variables.

2.2 Raw Data

Raw data refers to the originally collected data which has not been modified or transformed. Information collected through censuses, surveys, experiments and observations is known as raw data.

2.3 Types of Data According to Source

There are two types of data:

  • Primary Data
  • Secondary Data

Primary Data

Primary data are collected directly by the investigator for a specific purpose through observation, measurement or experimentation.

Methods of Collecting Primary Data

  • Direct personal interviews
  • Indirect oral interviews
  • Information from correspondents
  • Mailed questionnaire method
  • Schedules through enumerators
  • Telephonic interviews

Secondary Data

Secondary data are compiled from records collected by others. These data are usually available in published or unpublished sources.

Sources of Secondary Data

  • Government publications
  • Committee reports
  • Research publications
  • Trade and technical journals
  • Chambers of commerce and banks
  • International organizations

Difference Between Primary and Secondary Data

Primary Data Secondary Data
Collected by investigator directly Collected from records of others
Original data Not original
More reliable Less reliable
Expensive and time consuming Less expensive and faster
Raw material Finished product

Grouped and Ungrouped Data

Grouped Data

When data values are arranged into class intervals to simplify analysis, it is called grouped data. Example: 0–10, 11–20, 21–30

Ungrouped Data

Data that remain in their original form without grouping. Example: 2, 4, 10, 15, 21

2.4 Variable

A variable is a characteristic that varies from observation to observation.

Examples:
  • Temperature
  • Plant height
  • Crop yield
  • Soil nitrogen content

Observations (Variate)

The specific numerical values assigned to variables are called observations. Example: Crop yield = 30 kg

2.5 Types of Variables

Quantitative Variables

Variables expressed numerically such as height, weight, and number of seeds.

Qualitative Variables

Variables expressed as categories rather than numbers. Examples include color, taste, intelligence, and social class.

Continuous Variables

Variables that can take any value within a range. Examples:

  • Height
  • Weight
  • Temperature
  • Rainfall

Discrete Variables

Variables that take only specific integer values. Examples:

  • Number of students
  • Number of seeds
  • Number of leaves

2.6 Population

Population refers to the complete set of all items or individuals under investigation.

Examples:
  • Total students in a college
  • Total houses in a village
  • Total books in a library

Census

When every unit of the population is studied, the process is called census or complete enumeration.

Parameter

A parameter is a numerical measure describing a population characteristic. Examples:

  • Population Mean (μ)
  • Population Standard Deviation (σ)
  • Population Correlation (ρ)

2.7 Sample

A sample is a small portion of the population selected for analysis.

Sample Survey

When only a part of the population is investigated, it is called a sample survey.

Statistic

A statistic is a numerical measure describing a sample. Examples:

  • Sample Mean (x̄)
  • Sample Standard Deviation (s)

2.8 Nature of Data

Data may be classified based on time and place.

Time Series Data

Data collected over time. Example: Year-wise rainfall data.

Spatial Data

Data related to location. Example: District-wise rainfall data.

Spatio-Temporal Data

Data related to both time and location. Example: Monthly rainfall data for different districts.
