10.1 Introduction

Measures of central tendency and dispersion are not sufficient to describe the shape of a distribution. Two distributions may have the same mean and standard deviation but differ in shape. Skewness helps identify asymmetry, and kurtosis describes the peakedness of the curve.

10.2 Definition

Skewness: Refers to asymmetry in a frequency distribution.

10.3 Symmetrical Distribution

  • Bell-shaped curve
  • Mean = Median = Mode
  • Equal spread on both sides

10.4 Asymmetrical Distribution

If a distribution is not symmetrical, it is called skewed. Mean, median, and mode differ.

10.5 Types of Skewness

Positive Skewness

  • Tail extends to the right
  • Mean > Median > Mode

Negative Skewness

  • Tail extends to the left
  • Mean < Median < Mode

Zero Skewness

  • Perfect symmetry
  • Mean = Median = Mode

10.6 Tests of Skewness

  • Mean, Median, Mode are unequal
  • Quartiles are not equidistant
  • Curve is not bell-shaped
  • Unequal deviations

10.7 Measures of Skewness

Karl Pearson’s Method

Sk = (Mean − Mode) / SD
Sk = 3(Mean − Median) / SD
  • Range: -1 to +1
  • 0 → Symmetrical
  • + → Positive skew
  • - → Negative skew

Bowley’s Method

Sk = (Q3 + Q1 − 2Median) / (Q3 − Q1)
  • Based on quartiles
  • Useful when mode is undefined

Kelly’s Method

Sk = (P90 + P10 − 2P50) / (P90 − P10)

Skewness Based on Moments

β₁ = μ₃² / μ₂³

10.8 Moments

Moments are averages of deviations raised to powers.

  • μ₁ = 0
  • μ₂ = Variance
  • μ₃ = Skewness
  • μ₄ = Kurtosis

10.9 Kurtosis

Kurtosis measures the peakedness or flatness of a distribution.

Types of Kurtosis

Leptokurtic

  • Highly peaked

Mesokurtic

  • Normal distribution

Platykurtic

  • Flat curve

10.10 Measure of Kurtosis

β₂ = μ₄ / μ₂²
γ₂ = β₂ − 3
  • β₂ > 3 → Leptokurtic
  • β₂ = 3 → Mesokurtic
  • β₂ < 3 → Platykurtic
Summary:
  • Skewness → Shape (left/right)
  • Kurtosis → Peak (sharp/flat)
