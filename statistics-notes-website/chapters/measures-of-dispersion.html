9.1 Introduction

Measures of central tendency like Mean, Median and Mode indicate the central position of a dataset but do not describe the variability. Measures of dispersion explain how data values are spread.

Example:

Series A: 20, 20, 20 → Mean = 20

Series B: 5, 10, 45 → Mean = 20

Series C: 17, 19, 24 → Mean = 20

Though means are equal, distributions differ.

9.2 Definition

Dispersion is the extent to which individual values differ from the average.

9.3 Characteristics of Good Dispersion

  • Simple and easy to compute
  • Rigidly defined
  • Based on all observations
  • Suitable for algebraic treatment
  • Less affected by extreme values

9.4 Types of Dispersion

  • Absolute Measures
  • Relative Measures (Coefficient)

Range

Range = L - S

Where L = Largest value, S = Smallest value

Merits

  • Simple and easy
  • Useful in quick analysis

Demerits

  • Based only on extreme values
  • Highly affected by outliers

Quartile Deviation (Q.D.)

Q.D = (Q3 - Q1) / 2

Merits

  • Not affected by extreme values
  • Suitable for skewed distributions

Demerits

  • Ignores 50% of data
  • Not suitable for algebraic use

Mean Deviation (M.D.)

M.D = Σ|X - A| / n

Merits

  • Based on all observations
  • Simple to compute

Demerits

  • Ignores signs
  • Less accurate

Standard Deviation (S.D.)

σ = √(Σ(X - X̄)² / n)

Merits

  • Most widely used
  • Based on all data
  • Useful for further analysis

Demerits

  • Complex calculation
  • Affected by extreme values

Variance

Variance (σ²) = Σ(X - X̄)² / n

Coefficient of Variation (C.V.)

C.V = (σ / X̄) × 100

Used to compare variability between datasets. Lower C.V indicates more consistency.

Key Relationships

  • Q.D = (2/3) S.D
  • M.D = (4/5) S.D
  • S.D > M.D > Q.D
